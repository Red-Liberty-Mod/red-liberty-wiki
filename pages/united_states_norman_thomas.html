    Norman Mattoon Thomas is the current president of the United States, serving since March 4th, 1937. Thomas previously was involved in New York politics, including serving as the state's 43rd governor for two years.

    Early Years

    Thomas was the oldest of six children, born November 20, 1884, in Marion, Ohio, to Emma Williams (née Mattoon) and Weddington Evans Thomas, a Presbyterian minister. Thomas had an uneventful Midwestern childhood and adolescence, helping to put himself through Marion High School as a paper carrier for the Marion Daily Star. The summer after he graduated from high school his father accepted a pastorate at Lewisburg, Pennsylvania, which allowed Norman to attend Bucknell University. He left Bucknell after one year to attend Princeton University, the beneficiary of the largesse of a wealthy uncle by marriage. Thomas graduated magna cum laude from Princeton in 1905.

    After some settlement house work and a trip around the world, Thomas decided to follow in his father's footsteps and enrolled in Union Theological Seminary. He graduated and was ordained as a Presbyterian minister in 1911. After assisting the Rev. Henry Van Dyke at the fashionable Brick Presbyterian Church on Manhattan's Fifth Avenue, Thomas was appointed pastor of the East Harlem Presbyterian Church, ministering to Italian-American Protestants.

    Presidency (1933-)

    Thomas was sworn in as president on March 4, 1933. His cabinet reflected his desire to balance the inner factions of his party, and to represent a variety of tendencies of socialist thought.

    New Liberty Program

    Combining elements of democratic socialism, Christian ethics, and progressive reform, the program sought to stabilize the economy while restructuring American society along more equitable lines. Major initiatives included federal intervention in agriculture and industry, the promotion of worker-owned cooperatives, expanded public works, banking and credit reform, and early welfare provisions. The administration also pursued ambitious labor protections—such as workplace safety standards, reduced working hours, and the abolition of child labor—alongside civil rights efforts framed through a moral and religious lens. While the New Liberty program succeeded in alleviating the worst effects of the Depression and restoring a measure of economic stability, it provoked fierce opposition from conservative politicians, business interests, and reactionary groups, contributing to political polarization and the rise of organized extremist resistance later in the decade.

    Civil Rights

    The Thomas administration’s civil rights program represented one of the most ambitious federal efforts toward racial equality since the reconstruction period.

    Foreign Policy

    In foreign affairs, the Thomas administration advanced a policy often described as moral internationalism, seeking to balance anti-imperial principles with the realities of a deteriorating global order. Rejecting traditional great-power politics, Norman Thomas emphasized diplomatic mediation, arms limitation, and economic cooperation, particularly within the Western Hemisphere. Initiatives such as expanded Pan-American cooperation, development assistance programs, and the promotion of cooperative economic structures abroad aimed to create a stable “community of nations” resistant to both fascism and imperial domination.
